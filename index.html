Scoring Methodology

Each preclinical ASD model is scored on three validity dimensions (1–5) and assigned a translatability rating, following frameworks established by Silverman et al. (2010), Crawley (2004), and Nestler & Hyman (2010). Scores reflect consensus from published literature, not individual studies.

Face Validity
Does the model display ASD-like behavioural phenotypes across the three core domains: (1) social interaction, (2) restricted/repetitive behaviours, and (3) communication?
  • 5 — Robust, reproducible deficits in all three domains across multiple labs
  • 4 — Strong deficits in ≥2 domains, replicated
  • 3 — Moderate or domain-restricted phenotype
  • 2 — Mild or inconsistent phenotype
  • 1 — Minimal or no ASD-like behavioural phenotype
Construct Validity
Does the model share genetic, neurobiological, or circuit-level mechanisms with human ASD?
  • 5 — Known ASD-causative gene/CNV; mechanism characterised; aligns with human neuropathology
  • 4 — Strong genetic or mechanistic link; some human support
  • 3 — Plausible mechanism; gene associated but not confirmed causal
  • 2 — Weak genetic link or indirectly related mechanism
  • 1 — Mechanism uncharacterised or not related to known ASD biology
Predictive Validity
Do interventions that rescue the model's phenotype show benefit in human ASD or the corresponding syndrome?
  • 5 — Intervention identified in model is effective in human trials
  • 4 — Strong evidence of translation; at least one positive trial
  • 3 — Partial translation; mixed human evidence
  • 2 — Preclinical rescue achieved but clinical trials failed or not done
  • 1 — No predictive relationship established
ScoreInterpretationExample
5Confirmed clinical efficacyTrofinetide for Rett syndrome (FDA 2023)
4Strong translational signalRapamycin in TSC; everolimus (approved)
3Partial / mixed translationOxytocin trials in ASD — inconsistent
2Preclinical rescue failed in humansmGluR5 antagonists in FXS (Phase 3 failures)
1No clinical dataMany newer genetic models
Important caveat: Predictive validity for ASD models is substantially lower than for epilepsy models. The heterogeneity of ASD, poor biomarker availability, and challenges in clinical trial design mean that preclinical rescue data should be interpreted cautiously. A predictive validity score ≥3 reflects meaningful translational signal, not guaranteed clinical efficacy.
Non-mammalian models (zebrafish & Drosophila)

Zebrafish (Danio rerio) and fruit fly (Drosophila melanogaster) models are included in this database because they offer capabilities not available in rodents: transparent larvae enabling live circuit imaging, rapid generation times allowing high-throughput drug screening, and unparalleled genetic tools. However, their validity profiles differ systematically from mammalian models:

DimensionWhy scores differ
Face validityTypically 2–4. Zebrafish shoaling and Drosophila courtship are valid social readouts but are not equivalent to mammalian social cognition. Behavioural homology decreases with evolutionary distance.
Construct validityOften 4–5. Gene conservation across vertebrates (zebrafish) and invertebrates (Drosophila) is high for ASD-associated genes. Molecular pathway homology is the primary strength of these models.
Predictive validityVariable (2–4). Zebrafish drug responses can translate when the molecular target is conserved (e.g., rapamycin in TSC). Drosophila pharmacology is less predictive but has guided mammalian studies (e.g., lithium in Fragile X).
TranslatabilityLow–moderate for most. A low translatability rating for a zebrafish or fly model does not mean the model is uninformative — it reflects that additional mammalian validation is always required before clinical application.
How to read non-mammalian validity scores: A face validity score of 2 in a zebrafish model is not equivalent to a score of 2 in a rodent model. Zebrafish and Drosophila models should be assessed on their specific strengths — genetic tractability, throughput, and mechanistic insight — rather than penalised for behavioural differences that reflect evolutionary distance, not scientific quality.
Translatability Rating

High — model addresses a clearly defined molecular target with a tractable clinical path (e.g., FDA-approved indication exists).
Moderate — mechanistically relevant with some clinical data but mixed translation track record.
Low — poor genetic fidelity, unknown mechanism, or consistent clinical trial failures.
